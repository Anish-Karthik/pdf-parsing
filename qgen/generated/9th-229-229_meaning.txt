This is a well-structured lesson plan about a verse from the *Yasodhara Kavya*, focusing on the theme of righteousness (அறம்). Here are some suggestions for improvement:

**Enhancements:**

* **Expand on the "Karppavai Kattathin..." section:**  The comparison between "அறம் செய விரும்பு" (Desire to do good) and "ஆக்குவது ஏதெனில் அறத்தை ஆக்குக" (If you create something, create righteousness) is good, but could benefit from deeper analysis.  Consider adding questions to stimulate classroom discussion:
    * What is the difference in the tone and approach between the two phrases?  One is a general principle, the other a more directive command.
    * How do these phrases relate to the other lines in the verse (regarding anger, wisdom, and vows)?
    * Can students find modern-day examples that illustrate these principles?
    * What are the potential consequences of ignoring these principles?

* **Add a section on Literary Devices:** The verse uses parallel structure (all lines begin with a verb followed by "ஏதெனில்").  Pointing this out enhances understanding of the poem's structure and impact.

* **Contextualize the *Yasodhara Kavya* further:** Briefly explain the significance of the *Yasodhara Kavya* within the broader context of Tamil Sangam literature and Jain philosophy.  Why is it considered a significant text? What are its unique characteristics?

* **Visual Aids:** Suggest the use of visual aids in the classroom—images related to the themes of righteousness, anger, wisdom, and vows.

* **Activity Suggestions:** Include more specific activities beyond just a general discussion. For example:
    * Students could write their own short verses using the same parallel structure.
    * A role-playing activity illustrating a situation where characters choose between righteous and unrighteous actions.
    * A creative writing prompt:  How might Yasodharan have applied these principles in his life?

* **Vocabulary Enrichment:**  Provide definitions for less common words like "வியங்கோள்" and "வினைமுற்று."

* **Clarify the "சமண முனிவர்" statement:**  Instead of simply stating it's attributed to a Jain monk, offer a bit more nuanced explanation.  Is this a widely accepted view? Are there alternative attributions?
