Imagine a muscle fiber as a chain of tiny units called sarcomeres. These are like mini-engines responsible for muscle movement. They are made up of two main types of protein filaments:

  • Thin Filaments (Actin): Anchored at the edges of the sarcomere, these filaments slide during contraction.
  • Thick Filaments (Myosin): Located in the center, these filaments have "heads" that bind to actin, pulling them together.

In a relaxed state, the H-zone is the central, lighter region within the sarcomere where only myosin filaments are present. This means there's no overlap with the actin filaments.

When a muscle contracts, the myosin heads pull on the actin filaments, shortening the sarcomere and reducing the H-zone.

Therefore, in a relaxed sarcomere, the H-zone will only contain myosin proteins.
